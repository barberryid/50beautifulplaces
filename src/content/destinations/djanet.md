## Why it is beautiful

Djanet is the gateway to Tassili n'Ajjer, one of the largest concentrations of prehistoric rock art in the world — tens of thousands of engravings and paintings spread across a sandstone plateau the size of Scotland. The landscape ranges from stone forests and natural rock arches to sand seas and canyon systems, all inhabited by Tuareg communities whose connection to this desert runs back thousands of years. Desert nights here have almost no light pollution; the Milky Way is architectural. This is expedition travel rather than sightseeing — the reward is immersion in one of Earth's great ancient landscapes.

## 10 practical tips to help you decide

These tips are designed to help you decide whether Djanet fits your time, budget, comfort level, and travel style.

1. **For desert photographers, geology lovers, and travellers who want expedition-style immersion — not those looking for independent or easy travel.** Djanet suits people who want Saharan scale, Tassili rock art, Tuareg culture, and desert camping far from infrastructure. All visits require an organised guide and logistics; independent travel in southern Algeria is not feasible without formal arrangements. Skip it if you want city-style flexibility, guaranteed comfort, or a destination you can plan without specialist support.

2. **October to March only. April to September is extremely hot.** Djanet sits at the edge of the central Sahara where summer temperatures regularly exceed 45°C. The cooler desert season runs October to March, with pleasant daytime temperatures of 15–30°C and cold nights. October and March are shoulder months — warmer but manageable for experienced travellers. November to February is the optimal window: reliable temperatures, clear skies, and the best conditions for multi-day plateau camping.

3. **Fly Algiers to Djanet via Air Algérie — all southern travel requires a licensed local operator.** Air Algérie connects Algiers (ALG) to Djanet (DJG) directly. International visitors fly into Algiers first. Algeria requires a visa for most Western nationals — the process is not trivial and typically requires a letter of invitation or operator support. All Tassili n'Ajjer visits must be arranged through a licensed operator with registered Tuareg guides and formal permits. Allow 6–8 weeks ahead for logistics and arrange your visa through your operator or an Algerian embassy.

4. **Allow four to seven days minimum — a rushed visit misses the entire point.** The Tassili plateau requires multi-day circuits to reach the significant rock art sites, canyons, and erg landscapes. A one- or two-day trip from Djanet town barely reaches the surface. A four-day circuit covers the main Tassili highlights; seven days allows Erg Admer, Tikobaouine, and more remote art areas. The value of Djanet is cumulative: changing desert light, camp evenings, and deep landscape require time to absorb.

5. **Desert camp accommodation throughout — no hotels in the field.** Tassili circuit trips involve sleeping in Tuareg-guided desert camps: tents or sleeping under the stars on sand platforms. Djanet town has guesthouses for nights before and after. Food, camping equipment, and water are included in well-run packages. Cold desert nights require warm layers — temperatures can drop near freezing even after hot days. Pack a warm sleeping bag and layers regardless of the forecast.

6. **Costs are dominated by the tour package — Djanet itself is inexpensive.** A well-organised 5–7 day Tassili circuit with transport, guide, camping, and meals typically runs €150–300 per person per day depending on group size and operator. Smaller groups pay more per head. The tour cost covers most expenditure; personal budget beyond that is minimal. The main additional expense is the international flight to Algiers and the domestic connection to Djanet.

7. **Check your government's current Algeria advisory carefully — southeastern Algeria has specific risk areas.** The [UK FCDO advises against all travel within 30 km of Algeria's borders with Mali, Niger, and Libya](https://www.gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/algeria), and Djanet sits in the far southeast near these borders. The [US State Dept rates Algeria as Level 2: Exercise Increased Caution](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/traveladvisories/traveladvisories/algeria-travel-advisory.html), with Level 4 zones near border areas. Confirm the current advisory for the specific Tassili access areas with your operator before booking, and choose an operator with current local knowledge and security protocols.

8. **The prehistoric rock art is the cultural centrepiece — Tassili holds one of the world's great collections.** Tassili n'Ajjer's UNESCO listing rests primarily on an estimated 15,000+ engravings and paintings spanning 12,000 years — images of elephants, giraffes, and hippos from when the Sahara was green, through pastoral and later Tuareg periods. Major art areas include Iheren, Jabbaren, and Sefar. Visiting without a guide risks damaging irreplaceable engravings; all access to art sites must be with a registered guide who understands the conservation protocols.

9. **Desert nights at Djanet have some of the darkest skies accessible to international travellers.** The Tassili plateau has essentially no light pollution, and clear desert nights reveal the full Milky Way with a density of stars most urban travellers have never experienced. Plan at least one night sleeping away from camp fires on the open plateau. This is a secondary benefit of the camping-based circuit, but for many visitors it becomes one of the defining memories.

10. **Combine Djanet with Algiers and the northern tell if your visa and time allow.** Algeria has more than desert. Algiers has a magnificent Ottoman Casbah (UNESCO World Heritage), colonial boulevards, and a Mediterranean seafront. The Roman ruins at Timgad and Djémila in the north rank among the best-preserved in the world. If you are making the effort of obtaining an Algerian visa and flying to Djanet, a few days in the north before or after turns a single expedition into a fuller picture of one of Africa's most undervisited countries.
